# Brief: Reframing Organizations — Bolman & Deal (2017)

## Core Thesis

Organizations are complex, ambiguous, and paradoxical. Leaders fail when they rely on a single perspective. By "reframing" — viewing the same situation through four distinct lenses (Structural, Human Resource, Political, Symbolic) — leaders gain the versatility needed to diagnose problems accurately and design effective responses. The book is both a theory of organizations and a practical toolkit for leadership artistry.

---

## The Four-Frame Model (Core Framework)

Each frame is a mental model — a set of assumptions and lenses for seeing what is really going on in an organization.

### 1. Structural Frame

| Aspect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Metaphor** | Organization as factory / machine |
| **Central concepts** | Rules, roles, goals, policies, technology, environment, hierarchy, division of labor, coordination, formalization |
| **Key assumption** | Organizations exist to achieve goals; rational structure and clear division of work increase efficiency |
| **Leadership image** | Social architect — design structure for clarity and alignment |
| **When it works** | Clear goals, stable environment, measurable output |
| **When it fails** | Over-organization (rigidity, bureaucracy); ignoring human needs or political dynamics |

**Key sub-topics:**
- Vertical vs. lateral coordination
- Structural configurations (simple, machine, professional, divisional, adhocracy — per Mintzberg)
- Restructuring: realigning roles and relationships to fit strategy and environment
- Symptoms of structural deficiency: low morale, missed deadlines, unclear responsibilities, conflict

### 2. Human Resource Frame

| Aspect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Metaphor** | Organization as family |
| **Central concepts** | Needs, skills, relationships, motivation, participation, empowerment, human needs (Maslow, McGregor Theory X/Y, Argyris), care, growth |
| **Key assumption** | Organizations exist to serve human needs; when individual and organization are well-matched, both benefit |
| **Leadership image** | Catalyst / servant leader — empower, support, enable |
| **When it works** | Empowered employees, high commitment, alignment of individual and org values |
| **When it fails** | Codependency; underperformance due to excessive niceness; ignoring structural discipline or power realities |

**Key sub-topics:**
- Theory X vs. Theory Y management assumptions
- The "fit" between people and organization
- Participation and involvement as drivers of commitment
- Interpersonal dynamics, group process, conflict resolution
- Human resource practices: hiring, onboarding, training, career development, diversity

### 3. Political Frame

| Aspect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Metaphor** | Organization as jungle / arena |
| **Central concepts** | Power, conflict, coalitions, bargaining, scarce resources, interests, advocacy, negotiation, politics |
| **Key assumption** | Organizations are coalitions of diverse interests; resources are scarce; conflict is natural and inevitable |
| **Leadership image** | Advocate / negotiator — build coalitions, negotiate, manage conflict |
| **When it works** | Realistic diagnosis of power dynamics; effective advocacy for priorities |
| **When it fails** | Cynicism, backstabbing, paralysis from endless conflict; power struggles that override mission |

**Key sub-topics:**
- Sources of power: position, expertise, relationships, control of resources, symbolic power
- Mapping the political terrain: interests, power distribution, stakes
- Coalition building: creating alliances; negotiation and bargaining strategies
- Conflict management: when to avoid, accommodate, compete, compromise, collaborate
- Ethics of power: the difference between political awareness and Machiavellian manipulation
- Organizations as "organized anarchy" (Cohen, March, Olsen garbage can model)

### 4. Symbolic Frame

| Aspect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Metaphor** | Organization as theater / temple / carnival |
| **Central concepts** | Culture, meaning, belief, faith, ritual, ceremony, stories, myths, heroes, symbols, vision, charisma |
| **Key assumption** | Organizations are cultures; what matters most is not what happens but what it means; meaning is created through symbols and stories |
| **Leadership image** | Prophet / poet / artist — inspire, create meaning, shape culture |
| **When it works** | Strong cohesive culture, shared purpose, resilient through uncertainty |
| **When it fails** | Empty ritual, cynicism, culture that stifles adaptation; "groupthink"; symbolic gestures without substance |

**Key sub-topics:**
- Organizational culture: how beliefs, values, and practices are enacted
- Rituals and ceremonies as expressions of core values
- Stories and storytelling as vehicles for transmitting culture
- Heroes and heroines as embodiments of organizational values
- Vision as a symbolic act: defining and communicating purpose
- Charisma in leadership: the interplay of leader, followers, and context
- The shadow side of symbolism: manipulation, cults, empty pageantry

## Chapter Structure (6th Edition)

### Part I: Making Sense of Organizations
1. **Introduction:** The Power of Reframing
2. **Simple Ideas, Complex Organizations** — Why organizations are paradoxical and why single-frame thinking fails
3. **Getting It Together with the Four Frames** — Overview of each frame and when to use them

### Part II: The Structural Frame
4. **Organizations as Factories: Getting the Job Done** — Foundations of structural theory (Taylor, Weber, Fayol)
5. **Structuring and Restructuring** — Design configurations, vertical/lateral coordination
6. **Organizing in the Internet Age** — Networks, virtual organizations, matrix structures, the impact of technology

### Part III: The Human Resource Frame
7. **Organizations as Families: Nurturing People** — Human needs, motivation theories, McGregor, Maslow, Argyris
8. **Improving Human Resource Management** — Hiring, training, feedback, involvement, diversity
9. **Interpersonal Dynamics and Group Process** — Communication, group development, conflict, self-managing teams

### Part IV: The Political Frame
10. **Organizations as Jungles: Navigating Power and Conflict** — Sources of power, political dynamics, conflict
11. **The Manager as Politician** — Building alliances, networking, negotiating, ethical politics
12. **Organizations as Political Arenas and Political Agents** — Organizations as political actors in their environment

### Part V: The Symbolic Frame
13. **Organizations as Theaters: Understanding Culture and Meaning** — Culture as concept, symbols, stories, rituals
14. **Organizational Culture in Action** — How culture shapes strategy and performance; managing culture
15. **Organization as Theater and Spirit** — The spiritual dimension: meaning, faith, soul at work

### Part VI: Leadership and Integration
16. **Integrating the Frames: Leadership and the Art of Reframing** — How to use all four frames together; matching frame to situation
17. **Reframing in Action: Possibilities and Pitfalls** — Case studies applying multiple frames
18. **Reframing Leadership** — Leadership as artistry; image and reality of leaders
19. **Reframing Change** — Change through each frame: structural redesign, HR capacity building, political coalition building, symbolic meaning-making
20. **Reframing Ethics and Spirit** — Ethical leadership; the spiritual side of leading with soul

### Appendices
- **Appendix:** The Best of Organizational Studies — Citation analysis of most influential works
- **Epilogue:** Artistry, Choice, and Leadership — The meta-lesson: leadership as integrated, reflective practice

## Relevance to Ledelse 60:2

### Direct Structural Relationship
The company background explicitly states: *"Strukturen for intervjuet tar utgangspunkt i denne modellen"* (the interview structure is based on this model). This means:

1. **The 60 diagnostic questions are organized around the four frames** — each question probes leadership's perception within one of the four perspectives (Structural, Human Resource, Political, Symbolic)

2. **No single "right answer" philosophy maps directly to reframing theory** — Bolman & Deal argue that no frame is universally correct; different situations call for different frames. This directly supports the Ledelse 60:2 principle of "no scoring, no right/wrong, no facit"

3. **Comparative analysis over absolute scoring** — The four frames allow comparison of how different leaders within the same group see the same organization. Divergence in frame preference is itself diagnostic — a leadership team that over-uses the Structural frame and under-uses the Symbolic frame may have blind spots

### Frame-by-Frame Mapping to 60:2 Question Categories

| Bolman & Deal Frame | What It Probes in Ledelse 60:2 Context | Sample Diagnostic Angle |
|---------------------|---------------------------------------|----------------------|
| **Structural** | How is the organization designed? Are roles, goals, policies, and coordination mechanisms clear and aligned? Are there structural barriers to compliance and control? | "Is the compliance structure enabling or bureaucratic? Do formal systems support or hinder the work?" |
| **Human Resource** | How are people treated? Is there alignment between individual needs and organizational requirements? Is there trust, motivation, and participation? | "Do employees feel valued? Is there psychological safety? Is leadership present and visible?" |
| **Political** | How is power distributed and exercised? Are there unspoken conflicts, hidden agendas, or coalition dynamics? Who wins and loses from current arrangements? | "Are there power struggles blocking compliance? Is there advocacy for different priorities? Are resources allocated fairly?" |
| **Symbolic** | What is the culture? What stories, rituals, and symbols define the organization? Is there a shared sense of purpose and meaning? | "What does the organization stand for? Are there meaningful rituals or empty ceremony? Is the vision alive or just a poster on the wall?" |

### Why This Matters for the Ledelse 60:2 Method
- The interview process itself is a **reframing exercise** — it asks leaders to look at their own organization through different lenses
- The **reflection process is as important as the report** — this mirrors Bolman & Deal's emphasis on artistry, choice, and reflective practice
- The output (comparative insights across frames) gives leaders a **multiframe diagnosis** of their leadership culture — exactly what Bolman & Deal prescribe
