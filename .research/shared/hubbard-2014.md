# Brief: How to Measure Anything — Douglas W. Hubbard (2014)

## Core Thesis

"Anything" can be measured — including leadership, culture, risk, and other "intangibles" that managers assume are unmeasurable. The key is to understand measurement as "reduction of uncertainty" (not perfect knowledge), and to apply the right tools (Bayesian analysis, Monte Carlo simulation, Value of Information theory) to make better decisions under uncertainty. The book provides a rigorous, evidence-based methodology for quantifying what most organizations treat as unquantifiable.

---

## Key Concepts

### 1. The Measurement Definition
- **Measurement = reduction of uncertainty based on observation**
- You do not need perfect certainty to have a measurement — any observation that reduces uncertainty is a measurement
- **Critical implication:** If you can define what you mean by an "intangible" (e.g., "leadership maturity"), you can measure it — because you can determine what observations would reduce uncertainty about it
- The "myth of the intangible" — most things managers call "intangible" are simply poorly defined

### 2. The Five Measurement Myths (and Why They Are Wrong)

| Myth | Hubbard's Refutation |
|------|---------------------|
| "It can't be measured" | Anything that can be defined can be measured — measurement is just uncertainty reduction |
| "It's too vague to define" | Define it operationally: what would you observe if it were high vs. low? |
| "We don't have enough data" | You always have more data than you think (expert calibration, existing records, small samples) |
| "We need more data before we can decide" | Use Value of Information to determine if more data is worth the cost |
| "Measurements are too expensive" | Compare to the cost of the decision being wrong — measurement usually pays for itself |

### 3. The Applied Information Economics (AIE) Process
A structured methodology for quantifying decisions under uncertainty:

1. **Clarify the decision** — define the specific decision(s) the measurement will inform
2. **Define what you currently know** — express uncertainty as ranges, not point estimates
3. **Compute the value of additional information** — how much would perfect information be worth? (Expected Value of Perfect Information — EVPI)
4. **Determine what to measure** — focus measurement effort where it has the highest value
5. **Apply measurement methods** — expert calibration, sampling, Bayesian inversion, Monte Carlo simulation
6. **Update your model** — incorporate new information to reduce uncertainty
7. **Make the decision** — use the quantified analysis to choose the optimal course

### 4. Bayesian Methods
- **Bayes' Theorem** as the foundation for updating beliefs with evidence
- Prior → observation → posterior: any new information should update your estimate
- **Bayesian inversion** — working backward from observable effects to unobservable causes
- **Calibration** — training experts to produce well-calibrated probability estimates (eliminating overconfidence bias)
- The "Urn of Mystery" thought experiment: even vague knowledge can be expressed as a probability distribution

### 5. Value of Information (VoI)
- **EVPI (Expected Value of Perfect Information):** the maximum you should pay for more information
- **EVSI (Expected Value of Sample Information):** the value of a specific measurement or sample
- **Curvature matters** — the value of information depends on how much the decision outcome changes with new information
- **Decision-focused measurement:** only measure things that could change a decision — avoid measurement for its own sake
- **Practical rule:** If perfect information is worth less than the cost of obtaining it, stop measuring and decide now

### 6. Monte Carlo Simulation
- Method for modeling uncertainty by running thousands of scenarios
- Inputs are ranges (not point estimates); outputs are probability distributions of outcomes
- Reveals which variables drive the most uncertainty (sensitivity analysis)
- Enables "risk analysis" without requiring precise point forecasts

### 7. Expert Calibration
- Humans are consistently overconfident in their estimates
- **Calibration training** improves the accuracy of expert judgments significantly
- Key technique: ask for ranges (90% confidence intervals) and track hit rates
- A well-calibrated expert gets exactly 90% of their 90% confidence intervals correct
- **Calibration tests** (like the ones in the appendix) train people to assess their own uncertainty accurately

### 8. Measuring Intangibles: Practical Methods

| Intangible | Measurement Approach |
|------------|---------------------|
| **Risk** | Probability distributions; Monte Carlo simulation; Value at Risk |
| **Customer satisfaction** | Surveys + Bayesian updating; willingness-to-pay experiments |
| **Employee engagement** | Structured assessments calibrated to hard outcomes (turnover, productivity) |
| **Leadership effectiveness** | 360-degree assessments calibrated to team performance metrics |
| **Culture** | Language analysis (per Tribal Leadership); behavioral frequency data |
| **Innovation** | Option value analysis; R&D portfolio modeling |

## Relevance to Ledelse 60:2

### Primary Role: Measurement Methodology
Hubbard provides the **epistemological and methodological justification** for the Ledelse 60:2 approach. The method is a structured measurement of the "intangible" of leadership culture — and Hubbard's framework validates that this is both possible and valuable.

### Methodological Alignment

| Ledelse 60:2 Element | Hubbard Concept | How They Connect |
|----------------------|-----------------|------------------|
| 60 diagnostic questions | Measurement as uncertainty reduction | Each question is an observation that reduces uncertainty about leadership maturity |
| No scoring / no right-wrong | Measurement as range, not point | Hubbard says all measurement involves uncertainty; the 60:2 avoids false precision |
| Comparative insight (not facit) | Comparative analysis over absolute | Hubbard advocates comparing options, not optimizing against arbitrary targets |
| Reflection as value | Value of the measurement process itself | Hubbard's VoI framework would show that the 60:2 interview process generates valuable information even without a formal scorecard |
| Qualitative interview | Expert elicitation | The interview is a form of structured expert elicitation — Hubbard provides best practices for this |
| Leadership maturity as output | Quantifying an intangible | Hubbard would say: "define leadership maturity operationally, and you can measure it" — the 60:2 does exactly this |

### Why Hubbard Reinforces the 60:2 Philosophy
- The 60:2 does **not claim to produce a precise score** — it produces comparative insights. This aligns with Hubbard's view that measurement is uncertainty reduction, not perfect knowledge
- The 60:2 values **the process (reflection) as much as the output (report)** — Hubbard's EVPI framework would support that the mere act of structured inquiry generates value
- The approach is **decision-focused** — the goal is to help leaders make better decisions about their leadership culture, not to produce a meaningless benchmark
- **No false precision** — the 60:2 does not compute averages or scores; it surfaces perspectives. This is consistent with Hubbard's critique of arbitrary weighted-scoring methods

### Limitations in the 60:2 Context
- Hubbard would likely advocate for **more quantification** (e.g., calibrating the 60 diagnostic questions against hard outcomes like turnover, compliance metrics, financial performance)
- The 60:2 could benefit from Hubbard's **Monte Carlo simulation** to model the range of possible leadership maturity outcomes
- Hubbard's **calibration training** could improve the consistency of interviewers
- The "no right/wrong" philosophy conflicts with Hubbard's view that any measurement should reduce uncertainty about a specific decision — defining what decision the 60:2 supports more explicitly would strengthen the methodology
