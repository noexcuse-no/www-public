# Brief: Lead with LUV — Blanchard & Barrett (2011)

## Core Thesis

Great leadership is rooted in love — not sentimentality, but a genuine commitment to serving and valuing people. The book presents servant leadership as the most effective approach to creating lasting organizational success. Through a dialogue between Blanchard (leadership scholar) and Barrett (former President of Southwest Airlines, one of the most consistently profitable companies in aviation history), the book shows how love-driven leadership produces superior results across all stakeholders: employees, customers, investors, and communities.

---

## Key Concepts

### 1. Servant Leadership Is Love in Action
- Servant leadership is not about being "soft" — it is about **serving first, leading second**
- The leader's primary role is to support, enable, and develop their people
- Love in leadership = treating people with respect, dignity, and genuine care
- **Key distinction:** Servant leaders serve others' growth; self-serving leaders use others for personal gain

### 2. The Triple Bottom Line: Three Stakeholder Outcomes
| Outcome | Description | Key Metric |
|---------|-------------|------------|
| **Employer of Choice** | An organization where people want to work | Low turnover, high engagement, strong culture |
| **Provider of Choice** | Customers choose you over alternatives | Customer loyalty, repeat business, word-of-mouth |
| **Investment of Choice** | Investors want to invest | Strong financial returns, sustainable growth |

- These three outcomes are **interdependent** — neglecting one harms the others
- Being an Employer of Choice comes **first** — take care of employees, and they take care of customers and investors

### 3. A Compelling Vision
- Vision has three components:
  - **Significant Purpose** — why the organization exists beyond profit
  - **A Picture of the Future** — what success looks like (vivid, specific, inspiring)
  - **Clear Values** — how people should behave and treat each other
- Vision must be lived, not just posted on a wall

### 4. Maintaining a Strong Culture
- Culture is the **living expression of vision and values**
- At Southwest Airlines, culture was maintained through:
  - **Hiring for attitude** — skills can be taught; attitude cannot
  - **Celebration and recognition** — making success visible and shared
  - **Consistent communication** — values reinforced through every channel
  - **Leading by example** — executives model the culture
  - **Protecting the culture** — actively addressing behavior that undermines it
- **Cultural rituals** — traditions, celebrations, stories that reinforce identity

### 5. Defining Love in Leadership Context
- LUV is not romantic love — it is **agape love**: unconditional care for the well-being of others
- Love as leadership means:
  - Seeing people as whole human beings, not just role occupants
  - Being present and attentive
  - Providing honest feedback (caring enough to confront)
  - Celebrating successes and sharing grief
  - Creating psychological safety

### 6. Differences Between Self-Serving and Servant Leaders

| Dimension | Self-Serving Leader | Servant Leader |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------|
| **Focus** | Personal success | Others' success |
| **Power use** | Command and control | Empowerment and support |
| **Communication** | Tell, persuade, sell | Listen, understand, facilitate |
| **Credit** | Take credit for successes; blame others for failures | Give credit to others; take responsibility for failures |
| **Development** | Hoard information and opportunity | Develop others and share knowledge |
| **Decision-making** | Unilateral | Inclusive and participative |

### 7. Making Unions Your Business Partners
- Barrett's approach at Southwest: treat unions as **partners, not adversaries**
- Transparency, trust, and shared goals convert adversarial relationships into collaborative ones
- This is a concrete example of servant leadership applied to labor relations

### 8. Citizen of Choice — Community Responsibility
- Organizations have a responsibility beyond profit: to be good citizens
- Servant leadership extends beyond the organization to the wider community

## Relevance to Ledelse 60:2

### Primary Role: Human Resource Frame Operationalization
Blanchard & Barrett's servant leadership model operationalizes Bolman & Deal's Human Resource frame. Where the HR frame says "organizations serve human needs," Lead with LUV shows how this works in practice — at Southwest Airlines, over four decades of profitability while maintaining a culture of love and service.

### Integration Points

| Ledelse 60:2 Dimension | Lead with LUV Concept | Diagnostic Question Angle |
|------------------------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| Leadership presence | Servant-first vs. leader-first | "Do leaders see themselves as servants or commanders?" |
| Trust and safety | Love as leadership; psychological safety | "Do employees feel genuinely cared for?" |
| Culture strength | Maintaining a strong culture | "Is the culture intentional and actively maintained?" |
| Vision clarity | Compelling vision: purpose, picture, values | "Does the organization have a clear, lived vision?" |
| Employee engagement | Employer of Choice | "Would people choose to work here again?" |
| Motivation | Empowerment vs. control | "Are people motivated by inspiration or by fear?" |
| Celebration and feedback | Positive reinforcement; caring confrontation | "Does the organization celebrate wins and address problems openly?" |

### Why This Matters for the 60:2 Method
- The 60:2 interview structure explicitly probes the Human Resource dimension — questions about trust, motivation, relationships, and empowerment
- Understanding servant leadership gives the interviewer a **normative benchmark**: a leadership culture that reflects servant leadership principles is likely more mature than one that reflects self-serving leadership
- The emphasis on **vision, values, and culture** as interconnected elements mirrors the holistic diagnosis that Ledelse 60:2 aims to produce
- The "no scoring, no right/wrong" principle allows leaders to self-identify where they fall on the servant/self-serving spectrum without defensiveness
