# Brief: Power — Jeffrey Pfeffer (2010)

## Core Thesis

Performance alone does not guarantee career success or leadership effectiveness. Power is a skill — it can be learned, built, and wielded. Those who understand the principles of power and are willing to act on them consistently will outperform those who rely solely on merit, hard work, or hope that organizational politics don't exist. The book is a practical, evidence-based guide to acquiring, maintaining, and (when necessary) relinquishing power.

---

## Key Concepts

### 1. The Performance Fallacy
- Most people believe that if they do good work, success will follow automatically
- Empirical evidence shows weak correlation between performance and career outcomes
- **Key insight:** Performance is necessary but insufficient; you must also make your performance visible, build relationships, and manage perceptions

### 2. Personal Qualities That Build Power
- **Ambition and energy** — desire for power as a primary motivator
- **Focus and concentration** — single-minded pursuit of priorities
- **Self-knowledge** — understanding your own strengths, weaknesses, and blind spots
- **Confidence** — projecting certainty even when uncertain
- **Empathy** — understanding others' perspectives (not for altruism but for influence)
- **Capacity to tolerate conflict** — willingness to engage in difficult confrontations

### 3. Choosing Where to Start
- **Powerful positions** are those that are: visible, relevant to core business priorities, autonomous, and have high discretion
- **Unit power** comes from being in departments that solve critical problems, have unique expertise, or are hard to replace
- **Strategy:** Go where the power is — seek roles in high-visibility, high-impact areas

### 4. Getting In: Standing Out and Breaking Rules
- **Rule breaking** — strategic deviation from norms can signal power and independence
- **Visibility** — being known is essential; do not stay hidden behind good work
- **Personal branding** — craft a narrative that makes you distinctive
- **The Matthew Effect** — the rich get richer; early visibility attracts more visibility

### 5. Creating Resources
- **Resource creation** — build something from nothing (a project, a team, a network, a reputation)
- **Idiosyncrasy credits** — the right to deviate from norms earned through prior contributions
- **Ownership** — claim credit and build equity in your initiatives

### 6. Building Social Networks
- **Network structure matters** — weak ties (acquaintances) are more valuable than strong ties (close friends) for new opportunities (per Granovetter)
- **Structural holes** — positions that bridge disconnected groups provide power advantages
- **Network diversity** — breadth beats depth for information access and influence
- **Active networking** — it must be intentional, not incidental

### 7. Acting and Speaking with Power
- **Powerful behavior** — appearing confident, energetic, and in control
- **Use of space and time** — territorial markers (corner office, seating position) signal power
- **Interrupting** — powerful people interrupt more; being interrupted signals lower status
- **Emotional displays** — anger signals power; happiness signals warmth (but weaker status)

### 8. Reputation: Perception Is Reality
- **Reputation is a power asset** — once established, it precedes you
- **Impression management** — controlling how others perceive your competence and character
- **Consistency** — a consistent message builds credibility
- **Labeling** — be the first to define yourself before others define you

### 9. Overcoming Opposition and Setbacks
- **Persistence** — setbacks are inevitable; power is maintained by refusing to be defeated
- **Counter-power** — build alliances and resources to neutralize opposition
- **Turning enemies into allies** — strategic forgiveness and coalition building
- **Attribution** — control the story of setbacks (blame external factors, take credit for recoveries)

### 10. The Price of Power
- **Trade-offs** — power requires sacrifices (time, relationships, authenticity, work-life balance)
- **The corrupting effect** — power can lead to narcissism, overconfidence, ethical lapses
- **Psychological toll** — loneliness, distrust, constant vigilance

### 11. Why People Lose Power
- **Complacency** — once power is achieved, people stop doing what got them there
- **Overconfidence** — belief that power is permanent leads to risky behavior
- **Losing touch** — isolation from those below you erodes your base
- **Scandal or overreach** — hubris + visible failure = rapid fall

### 12. Power Dynamics: Good for Organizations?
- **Political skill correlates with performance ratings** — politically savvy managers are rated higher
- **Power distribution affects organizational health** — too centralized = rigidity; too diffuse = paralysis
- **Ethical power use** — power can be used for collective good or personal gain; the book advocates using it to get things done

## Relevance to Ledelse 60:2

### Primary Role: Political Frame Operationalization
Bolman & Deal's Political frame identifies that organizations are political arenas. Pfeffer's book is the **practical playbook for that frame**. Ledelse 60:2 draws on the Political frame to understand:
- How power is distributed within the leadership group
- Whether there are unspoken conflicts or hidden agendas
- How resources and influence are allocated
- Whether the leadership team functions as a coalition or a unified group

### Integration Points

| Ledelse 60:2 Dimension | Pfeffer Concept | Diagnostic Question Angle |
|------------------------|-----------------|--------------------------|
| Power distribution | Sources of power; structural power | "Who actually makes decisions — and who should?" |
| Conflict handling | Tolerance for conflict; negotiation | "How does the leadership team handle disagreement?" |
| Network/relationships | Social network building; coalitions | "Are leaders well-connected internally and externally?" |
| Resource allocation | Creating vs. consuming resources | "Are resources allocated based on merit, relationships, or power?" |
| Career/personal ambition | Personal qualities for power | "Do leaders demonstrate ambition, energy, and focus?" |
| Organizational politics | Political skill vs. performance | "Does the organization reward performance or politics?" |

### Why Pfeffer Matters for the 60:2 Philosophy
- The 60:2 approach creates a safe space for leaders to discuss **political realities** without judgment
- Recognizing that power dynamics exist (per Pfeffer) enables the interview to surface issues that "nice" leadership assessments miss
- The "no scoring, no right/wrong" principle aligns with Pfeffer's observation that **context determines what power strategy works** — there is no universal formula
